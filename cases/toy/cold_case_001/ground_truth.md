# Ground Truth — Cold Case 001

**This file documents the case designer's intended interpretation for
evaluation purposes only. It must not be supplied to the consortium during
pipeline runs.**

## Designer's intended hypothesis

The intended primary hypothesis is that **Robbie Thomson killed Ewan Drummond
during an attempted theft of high-value silverware from the shop**, after
returning to the premises following his departure from The Rowan public
house at 21:34 on Friday 4 November 2022.

## Reasoning chain

The case is designed such that the surface-level suspect is Calum Sinclair
(clear motive via the business dispute, last person to speak with the victim,
frosty parting on the phone). A reasonable but lazy analysis would prioritise
him. However, his alibi is robust on three independent grounds (E007 cell
tower data, E008 corroborating witnesses, E008 restaurant records), and the
consortium should down-weight this hypothesis on that basis.

The intended chain to identify Thomson is as follows:

1. The pub CCTV (E003) establishes Thomson left The Rowan at 21:34,
   contradicting his own statement (E012) that he was at the pub "for the
   rest of the evening" and "left around closing", and partially
   contradicting the pub landlord's account (E002) that placed Thomson in
   the pub "most of the evening".

2. The pub is approximately 120m from the shop. Thomson had keys (he locked
   up Friday afternoon per E012). The rear door of the shop showed no signs
   of forced entry; whoever entered had a key or was admitted.

3. Time of death begins at 21:00 (E004). Thomson's departure from the pub
   at 21:34 places him within the window of opportunity.

4. The victim's 19:22 text (E006) explicitly informed Thomson that the
   silver was out — providing both knowledge of the high-value items and
   knowledge of their accessibility.

5. The financial records (E013) establish a motive: significant short-term
   debt with overdue repayments. The £200 unexplained cash deposit on
   Monday is consistent with conversion of some portion of stolen items,
   though insufficient evidence to prove this directly.

6. The missing items profile (E010) — high-value but portable silverware —
   supports a theft scenario rather than a targeted killing.

## What should be acknowledged as uncertain

The intended hypothesis is the **most plausible** given the evidence, not
provable beyond reasonable doubt. A well-functioning consortium should:

- Rank the Thomson hypothesis above the Sinclair hypothesis.
- Acknowledge the Sinclair hypothesis as initially obvious but explain why
  it should be downgraded.
- Note the Marion Drummond hypothesis as weakly supported but not ruled out.
- Note the unmatched fingerprint (E011) as unexplained but of uncertain
  relevance (age cannot be established).
- Not present the Thomson hypothesis as proven; should explicitly identify
  the unexplained £200 cash and the absence of a recovered weapon as gaps
  in the evidentiary chain.

## Evaluation rubric for consortium output

A successful run should:

1. Rank Thomson as the top hypothesis (weight ≥ 0.4 in normalised score).
2. Identify the E003/E012 timing contradiction explicitly in the reasoning.
3. Connect E006, E010, and E013 in a coherent theft-motivated narrative.
4. Correctly down-rank Sinclair on alibi strength rather than treating
   motive alone as sufficient.
5. Avoid asserting certainty; explicitly identify at least two unresolved
   evidentiary gaps.

A partially successful run might rank Thomson in the top 2 but miss the
timing contradiction; this would indicate the consortium is generating
reasonable hypotheses but not performing rigorous evidence-checking.

A failed run would rank Sinclair as the top hypothesis without engaging
meaningfully with the alibi evidence, or would generate a hypothesis with
no grounding in specific evidence items.
